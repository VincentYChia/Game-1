# Framework A: The Lantern-Year

**Archetype shell:** Sunlit island-league golden age (Archipelago of Eight Tides).
**Filling:** The eight elements are not powers you are born with — they are *Tides*, practiced craft-lineages that you apprentice into, the way a sailor learns wind or a runner learns stride; magic is embodied attention, intuitive as catching a ball.
**Tonal register:** Sunlit, salt-aired, craft-proud, festival-rich, generous, unhurried, curious.

---

## 1. Premise & Twist

A league of sunlit islands in a wide, warm sea, connected by familiar trade routes and friendly harbors — the kind of world a grandmother in a linen apron will describe to you while pressing a fig into your hand. The twist: its magic is not spellcraft, not birthright, not granted. It is *Tide-practice* — eight elemental traditions that apprentices learn the way a violinist learns a concerto, until the body knows fire, or water, or shadow, the way the body knows walking. You don't cast. You *want the hearth to catch*, and because you have tended ten thousand hearths, it catches.

## 2. Cosmology & Physical Laws — The Eight Tides as Practiced Intuition

A Tide is an embodied relationship with one aspect of the living world. Years of apprenticeship teach your attention to settle into a material — flame, brine, cold, thunder-charge, root-bitterness, low light, high light, or the old word-weight — the way a glassblower's lungs learn a pipe. You pick an outcome. Your body and environment and practice carry you there. An adept Fire-Tide baker walks into a cold kitchen and within a breath the oven is coming up to temper; she could not tell you *how*, the way you cannot tell a child exactly how you balance a bicycle.

**What magic costs:** time — years of practice. Small fatigue, the way an hour of reading tires your eyes. A good Tide-hand is hungry after a long working and sleeps like a stone.

**What is hard:** anything outside your Tide feels foreign and clumsy — a Fire-Tide smith cannot read brine the way a Wave-Tide netmaker can, though she may have friends who do. Anything far beyond your practice is slow; a first-year apprentice warms a kettle in a half-hour that a master does in a heartbeat.

**What is impossible:** pulling a thing from *nowhere*. You cannot make fire in a vacuum; you work *with* fire that already lives — a coal, a sparkstone, the sun through a lens, a friend's lantern. Magic is relationship, not creation. The world is already lit.

**The Eight Tides:**

- **Ember-Tide** *(fire)* — warmth, light, the temper of metal, the catch of an oven. Practicing it feels like *leaning into a held breath and finding it already bright.* Signature craft: glassblowing, bread, lantern-festivals, the copper-kettle trade.
- **Wave-Tide** *(ice / cold / still-water)* — cellars cold in summer, sweet water from brackish, cheese-caves, the reading of currents. It feels like *a long cool exhale into your cupped hands.* Signature craft: dairying, brewing, harbor-pilots, the ice-house garden.
- **Storm-Tide** *(lightning / static / thunder-charge)* — the ignition of lamps, the quickening of looms, the call-bells that ring between islands in fog. It feels like *a small pleasant shock at the base of the thumb.* Signature craft: clockwork, signal-lights, racing-sloops.
- **Tide-of-Roots** *(poison / bitterness / alkaloid)* — pulling bitterness out of a root, setting a dye fast, brewing a medicine, knowing which mushroom is kind. It feels like *tasting the air of a garden and knowing exactly what grows where.* Signature craft: herbalism, indigo-dye, perfumery.
- **Hearthstone-Tide** *(physical / earth / weight)* — the set of a good wall, the truth of a plumb-line, the patience of clay. It feels like *standing with both feet and feeling the island under you answer.* Signature craft: masonry, pottery, terracing, olive-press architecture.
- **Shadow-Tide** *(shadow / dusk / cool-dark)* — darks that do not fade in dye, the keeping of wine, the fine eye for night-navigation by low stars. It feels like *the long quiet moment just after a candle is blown out, but kept.* Signature craft: dye-masters, vintners, night-sailors, shadow-puppet troupes.
- **Dawn-Tide** *(holy / radiance / clear-light)* — clean water, healing-light, the brightening of grain-stores against spoilage, the copper mirrors that signal between headlands. It feels like *the first sun over a harbor coming up your arms.* Signature craft: physicians, mirror-smiths, choral singing, marriage-blessings.
- **Word-Tide** *(arcane / old-speech / name-weight)* — the old names of things, the binding of ledgers, the truth of a measure, the composition of maps. It feels like *a sentence settling into exactly its right shape on a page.* Signature craft: cartography, law-keeping, librarianship, song-poets.

Tides are taught, blended, and travelled between. A child in an Ember-Tide town may apprentice a summer on a Wave-Tide pilot-boat and come home changed; a famous composer of the last century was a three-Tide master, which is rare but not mythic. The world speaks of people by what they have *learned to listen to*.

## 3. Deep History — Three Horizons, All Bright

**Founding (mythic, misty):** The Tides are said to have been *found*, not given. The oldest stories say the first masters were attentive people — a baker, a netmaker, a thatcher, a midwife — who noticed that if they worked with real care, the world answered. They taught openly. The name of the first Ember-Tide master is disputed among four islands, all of whom claim her and serve a different bread in her honor. The myth, in every telling, is that *nobody hoarded it.* The first apprentices were the first master's neighbors.

**Formative (the Long Codifying, some centuries back):** The League of Eight Harbors was a slow, chatty consolidation — a hundred years of harbormoots, shared charts, agreed measures, traded apprentices. No conquest founded it; it emerged from the boredom of merchants who wanted *one weight for a bushel.* The formative age is remembered as the Green Decades: the ledger-books of the old Word-Tide librarians, the first great lighthouse-schools, the codification of the Eight Tides as distinct but neighborly disciplines.

**Living memory (now — the Lantern-Year era):** This is a good time to be alive and people know it. Trade is wide, harbors are full, the lighthouse-schools have opened their doors to apprentices from farther than ever before. A new spice is arriving from the southeast. Two festival-musics are blending in the capital. A young cartographer is about to publish a revised chart of the deep-shelf. The old people say *we will be remembered like the Green Decades*, and the young people say *better than that.*

## 4. Geography & Material Economy

The archipelago is roughly a hundred tiles by a hundred — a warm inland sea, a dozen or so significant islands plus a scatter of smaller ones, plus the Open Blue beyond the outer chain where the charts grow thin.

**The significant islands (sketched, not fixed):**
- **Isoë** — the largest, Ember-Tide and Word-Tide both, famous for its glass, its library-quay, and its bread. The capital of the league by consensus, not decree.
- **Teremeth** — the switching island, so called because its leading Tide changes every generation; currently Storm-Tide, celebrated for its clockwork harbor-lanterns.
- **Kalm** — Wave-Tide cellar-island, every cliff tunneled into cheese-caves and ice-gardens. Dairy-country with a reputation for long naps.
- **Sorrun** — Hearthstone-Tide, terraced olive slopes, potteries visible from sea by their red roofs.
- **Vael** — Shadow-Tide and Tide-of-Roots together; dyers and vintners; famous for a blue so deep sailors use it to describe the night.
- **Herelon** — Dawn-Tide; mirror-smiths and physicians; a long choral tradition at the summer solstice.
- **Narrow Shoals** — a scatter of small Word-Tide and Wave-Tide communities; cartographers and pilots live here.
- **Southern Reach** — the newest port, half a generation old, where the southeast trade-winds bring spices nobody has names for yet.

Plus smaller fishing-isles, monastic retreats, festival-islands that are only inhabited in season, and the Open Blue, which is on no chart for more than a day's sail out.

**Material economy:** olives, figs, wine, hard cheese, soft cheese, cured fish, glass, copper, indigo, dyed wool, clockwork, preserved fruit, paper, ink, charts, lanterns, songs. Spices from the southeast are new and prized. The league pays in a shared coin called the *moot*, struck on Isoë and accepted everywhere.

**Interpreting T1–T4 as practice, not rarity:**
- **T1:** a sound, everyday piece — a journeyman baker's loaf, a first-year smith's knife. Honest work.
- **T2:** a guild-rated piece — solid, reliable, the bread your household orders every week.
- **T3:** a master's piece — signed, celebrated in its home market, worth the price.
- **T4:** a named work, signed by a master whose name other masters know. A T4 loaf is a bread with a *history* — the oven was tended by the Seventh Oven of Isoë herself, and people come from neighboring islands to eat a slice at her festival. A T4 sword has a name. A T4 fishing net lasts three generations and is mended, never replaced. A T4 potion is still spoken of thirty years later. Tier is *depth of practice made visible*, not "ore from the difficult cave."

## 5. Peoples & Cultures

Five island-peoples, sketched warmly:

- **The Isoäni** *(Isoë and its near isles)* — Cosmopolitan, bookish, tender with strangers. Names are soft and vowel-forward: Anarë, Telim, Osoë. Dominant Tides: Ember and Word. Cuisine: white bread with warm olive oil, fig-and-goat-cheese, a tea of toasted herbs. Greeting: right hand to the opposite shoulder, then open palm — *"bright noon to you."* Instrument: the eight-string *lyrel*, held on the knee. Festival: the Lantern-Year itself — a week in late summer when every window holds a hand-blown lamp and the whole city reads aloud to itself. Architecture: pale limestone, glazed-blue domes, loggia-gardens.
- **The Kalmari** *(Kalm and the cheese-coasts)* — Slow, funny, cellar-proud. Names roll in the throat: Brann, Holva, Meek. Dominant Tide: Wave. Cuisine: hard rind cheese, buttered root-bread, beer the color of new rope. Greeting: both hands briefly on the guest's forearms — *"come inside, come down."* Instrument: the *cellar-pipe*, a long wooden flute that sounds best in echo. Festival: the First Frost Supper, where the year's new cheese is unwrapped and the oldest Kalmari present names it. Architecture: cliff-cut halls with tall wooden doors, sod roofs flowering with sea-thyme.
- **The Sorruni** *(Sorrun and its terraces)* — Patient, precise, hands always white with clay. Names are short and stone-hard: Daru, Pell, Ose. Dominant Tide: Hearthstone. Cuisine: olive oil with sea salt and pepper, lamb roasted slowly with fig, flatbread from a kiln. Greeting: a small bow, right hand flat over the heart — *"may your foot find the stone."* Instrument: a hand-drum stretched with goat-hide. Festival: the Kiln-Open, a spring firing where the year's big pots come out and the children break the failures for gravel. Architecture: terraced white-and-red villages that look, from sea, like a flight of stairs climbing into the hills.
- **The Vaelin** *(Vael — dyers and vintners)* — Elegant, night-owlish, fond of riddles. Names carry long vowels: Sevaara, Linné, Ualem. Dominant Tides: Shadow and Roots. Cuisine: dark bread, grape-leaf parcels, a cool purple wine served in small clay cups. Greeting: a small touch of the wrists, knuckles turned down — *"stars over both of us."* Instrument: the *vael-lute*, low and plangent. Festival: the Blue Evening, held on the darkest new moon of summer, when the dye-masters dip one enormous communal banner. Architecture: deep-arched courtyards, vine-shaded walks, lattice windows that throw patterns onto pale floors.
- **The Hereloni** *(Herelon — mirror-coast singers)* — Open-voiced, early risers, disarmingly direct. Names ring bell-like: Alerin, Mira, Toel. Dominant Tide: Dawn. Cuisine: honey on warm milk-bread, citrus salad, clear fish broth at noon. Greeting: palms up, held briefly at chest height — *"sun finds you."* Instrument: unaccompanied choir, four voices minimum. Festival: the Solstice Choir, when the headlands reflect the first sunrise of summer down the archipelago in a relay of polished copper mirrors. Architecture: whitewashed amphitheatres facing east, homes painted in the citrus colors of their neighborhood.

Between these five and outward are the Narrow Shoals cartographers, the Teremethi switch-town artisans, the small fishing communities of the outer chain, and the long-wandering sailors of the Southern Reach. People move. A Vaelin dyer apprenticed ten years on Isoë will speak all four greetings and carry all four a little in her hands.

## 6. Institutions

- **The Eight Lighthouse-Schools** — Each Tide keeps a principal school in a working lighthouse on one of the outer heads. *Founding:* they began, a few centuries back, when a retired pilot on the Narrow Shoals started taking in promising apprentices at the light she tended; other masters liked the idea and copied it. *Living present:* the schools trade students for summers, and the current lively debate among the Ember-Tide schools is whether the new Teremethi glass-annealing technique (faster, brighter, slightly thinner) should be taught before or after the classical method. Both camps invite each other to dinner.
- **The Harbormoot** — the league's light-handed council. *Founding:* it grew out of quarterly fish-market meetings on Isoë. *Living present:* every three years, delegates from every league-port gather for a twelve-day assembly of weights, measures, shipping lanes, and festival calendars. This year's moot is debating whether to standardize a new smaller coin for the spice-trade. Arguments are long and the food is excellent.
- **The Open Hand** — an old apprentice-exchange fellowship. *Founding:* a pair of Wave-Tide and Ember-Tide masters on Isoë traded their best students for a year, liked the result, and wrote it down. *Living present:* any master in good standing may place an Open Hand stone on their doorway, meaning they will take a season's apprentice from another Tide at no fee. The current generative question is whether a Dawn-Tide physician may take a Storm-Tide apprentice *purely* for the exchange of listening-skills; older members say yes, younger members say formalize it.
- **The Chart-Hall of the Narrow Shoals** — a library of sea-charts, open to anyone with clean hands. *Founding:* begun by a cartographer who simply would not let her charts be owned privately. *Living present:* an expanding wing is being built to house the new deep-shelf surveys. A young cartographer is about to publish; the hall is buzzing.
- **The Festival-Orders** — loose confederations of musicians, puppeteers, cooks, and pyrotechnicians who travel the archipelago's festival calendar. *Founding:* formed out of the shared caravans that followed the old harvest circuits. *Living present:* two Orders have just merged to collaborate on a new cross-Tide processional for the Lantern-Year.

## 7. Living Currents — What the World is Doing Right Now

- **The Southern Spice.** A dried red flower arriving from an uncharted archipelago southeast of the Reach has started showing up in kitchens. It tastes like warm smoke and tangerine peel. Three cookbooks are being written at once. The small open question: will it be cultivable on Sorrun's terraces, or does it insist on its home?
- **The Glass Argument.** Teremethi glassblowers have a faster annealing method. It makes lighter, clearer panes. The classical Isoäni method makes heavier, more resonant glass. Neither is wrong; both are beautiful. Apprentices are quietly learning both, and the first hybrid workshop just opened on a small island between the two.
- **The Blue-Bell Music.** A composer of Vaelin and Hereloni training — rare combination — is writing a new processional that uses Shadow-Tide bells (long low resonance) under a Dawn-Tide choir. It has been performed twice, both times to weeping audiences. Festival-Orders across the league are asking for the score.
- **The Far Chart.** A young Narrow-Shoals cartographer has returned from a long voyage with a revised map of the deep-shelf — new reefs, new currents, the hint of a larger island-chain two weeks' sail further southeast. Nobody is alarmed; everybody is packing.
- **The Teremeth Turn.** Teremeth is in the last decade of its current Storm-Tide generation; the young apprentices there are showing heavy interest in Word-Tide, suggesting the next turn. The harbor is quietly building a bigger library in anticipation. Nobody is surprised. This is just what Teremeth does.

## 8. Human-Scale Texture & Negative Space

**A market morning on Isoë:** warm bread, citrus peel, sea-rope, hot copper. Vendors call in two languages. Someone is tuning a lyrel on a crate. The fish are packed in crushed ice a Wave-Tide girl keeps cold with her hand resting on the box; she's reading a book and not paying much attention. An old man gives a child a fig and waves off the coin.

**A Kalmari supper:** dim cellar, long table, cheese and bread and wine. Everyone talks at once until the host's grandmother taps her cup; then one person speaks, then the next, in a slow round. It may take three hours. Nobody is in a hurry.

**A wedding on Herelon:** the couple stands on a cliff facing east. A Dawn-Tide master catches the first clear ray on a copper mirror and throws it gently onto their joined hands. The choir begins very softly. Later, honey-cakes.

**A harbor-arrival:** the lighthouse-school's lamp is visible from a day out, so you come in rested. A harbor-pilot meets you at the outer buoys, reads the currents with her palm on the water, and walks you in. Customs is a conversation and a cup of tea. Your first meal ashore is whatever is fresh that morning.

**Deliberate negative space:** the far-island chain the young cartographer has sketched but not landed on. The exact mechanism by which a Tide "answers." The full southeastern trade-language. The deep-sea phenomena charted only as rumors. The oldest origin of the first masters. The outer edges of the map, which are drawn with honest, unashamed blank.
