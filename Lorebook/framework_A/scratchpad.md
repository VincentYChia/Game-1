# Framework A: The Eight Courts and the Bronze Tide

**Archetype shell:** Sunlit mythic-heroic fantasy (Bronze-Age Living Mythology).
**Filling:** The eight elemental Courts are living, jealous cosmic houses whose favor must be *earned and spent*; the six crafting disciplines are priestly lineages that drifted into trades, and every T4 ingot, vial, or hull-plank is a ritual artifact whose power is the record of an unbroken oath.
**Tonal register:** Epic, oath-bound, sunlit, wonder-adjacent-to-danger, ancestor-present, shame-haunted, weathered-bronze.

## 1. Premise & Twist

A young world of island-leagues and headland city-states under a sky so close you can almost hear it breathing — and the eight elemental powers that hold up that sky are not distant gods but **Courts**: primordial houses with rivalries, fashions, and grudges, whose attention is a resource mortals must court, hoard, and never squander. Every named deed adds a line to a song someone somewhere is already singing. Every oath sworn cuts a thread into the weave that the Courts themselves read at dusk.

**The twist in one sentence:** In this world, *craft is liturgy that forgot it was liturgy* — the six great trades began as devotional rites to specific Courts, drifted over centuries into material work, and still hum with that old devotion when touched by someone whose oaths hold.

## 2. Cosmology & Physical Laws

### The weave

The world is held together by **standing** — the regard in which a Court holds a mortal, a lineage, a city, a harbor, a forge. Standing is not worship. A Court cares nothing for prayer alone. Standing is earned by: worthy deeds witnessed under open sky; offerings appropriate to that Court's nature; restraint where restraint is hard; and — above all — **oaths kept**. Standing is spent when a mortal asks something of the element: to light a cold forge, to quiet a storm long enough to land, to coax poison from a wound, to hold a blade's edge through one more blow.

Spend recklessly and the Court withholds. A fisher who curses the sea in bad weather may find nets empty for a season. A smith who lies about the provenance of an ingot may find the next ingot will not take heat. Magic is not a spell-list — it is a running account, and the Courts keep better books than any harbor-master.

### The eight Courts

Each Court has a face, a register, and a price.

- **The Stone-Court** (physical). Oldest, slowest, least forgiving. Speaks in weight, in mass, in the argument a mountain makes against a river. Patron of walls, of bones, of the covenant of standing still. Its price is stillness — you must hold your ground to ask it anything.
- **The Hearth-Court** (fire). Proud, domestic, jealous. It is not the wildfire; the wildfire is its ungovernable cousin. The Hearth-Court wants to be invited, named, fed. Its price is the small ritual done without shortcut.
- **The Rime-Court** (ice). Patient as widowhood. Remembers every grievance, will wait generations to settle one. Speaks in silence and in preservation. Its price is to keep a thing you would rather let go.
- **The Lash-Court** (lightning). Capricious, bright, short-tempered. Loves a bold stroke, hates a second thought. Its price is speed — hesitate and it has already left.
- **The Green-Court** (poison). Not evil; indifferent. The Court of rot, sap, ferment, venom, healing — because medicine and venom are one Court in different moods. Its price is honesty about appetite: do not ask the Green for more than you can digest.
- **The Veilwise Court** (arcane). Patron of names, of memory, of the shapes behind shapes. Loves a riddle, despises a lie. Its price is that a favor asked is a favor remembered — the Veilwise will call it back, at a moment of its choosing, years from now.
- **The Undersong Court** (shadow). The Court of what is true but not said: privacy, grief, sleep, ancestors, the held breath between lightning and thunder. Its price is silence — ask it something, and do not speak of the asking, ever.
- **The Dawn-Court** (holy). Young, proud, loud, covenantal. The Court of oaths sworn before witnesses, of sunlight falling on a named thing, of public honor. Its price is that you must mean it; a Dawn-Court favor bought with a hollow oath sours in the hand.

### Forbidden things

Three acts diminish *all* standing, not only with one Court:

1. **Oath-breaking that was witnessed.** An oath sworn in private can be renegotiated; an oath sworn in front of three or more breathing witnesses enters the weave, and breaking it is a thing every Court feels.
2. **Killing a guest.** Hospitality is older than the Courts; the Courts inherited it.
3. **Naming the dead without cause.** Names are threads. Pulling a dead name carelessly unravels more than you meant to.

Nothing else is forbidden in this absolute way. Murder, theft, cruelty — these cost standing with *some* Courts and gain it with others. The world is not moral in the modern sense. It is *accounted*.

### The six stats as cultural virtues

The things mortals are measured by:

- **STR** — *Heft.* What a body can carry and compel. Admired in quarry-folk and oar-pullers.
- **DEF** — *Standing-Under.* The old virtue of bearing weight without flinching. Measured at funerals, not in duels.
- **VIT** — *Breath-Hoard.* The wind in the chest; the years still owed to the ancestors.
- **LCK** — *Ancestor-Weight.* Fortune as inheritance — the press of well-remembered dead who still intercede.
- **AGI** — *Quickwit-of-the-Hand.* Admired in sailors, thieves, and dancers alike; no moral judgment attached.
- **INT** — *Name-Sight.* Not cleverness. The ability to see what a thing truly is, which is inseparable from knowing its true name.

## 3. Deep History (Stratified)

### Mythic/founding age — *the Long First Morning*

Before the Courts were eight, there were more, and the world was louder. The songs disagree on how many — some say twelve, some nineteen, one stubborn island sings thirty-three. What the songs agree on: a quarrel so bad it cracked the sky's first roof, and a Settling in which eight Courts remained and the rest were either bound, drowned, or folded into the eight as lesser moods. The drowned Courts are called **the Unhoused**, and they still exist — toothless, exiled — in deep water, in deep caves, in the hour before dawn. They can still grant favor. The price is always worse than they say.

From the Long First Morning also comes the **First Oath**: that mortals and Courts would deal with each other by *standing*, not by compulsion. No Court may simply *take* a mortal. A mortal must offer, and a Court must accept. This is the oldest law and the only one no Court has ever been known to break. Whether it is truly unbreakable or merely untested is a question priests do not like asked.

### Formative centuries — *the Bronze Tide*

Roughly four to six generations ago, a wave of metallurgical awakening spread across the island-leagues — bronze, refined tin, the first true smelting of hard iron in the southern continents. With it came the **Lineage Drift**: the six devotional crafts, which had been temple rites, became workshops. A hearth-priest of the Hearth-Court who had once sung a hymn over a blade now forged the blade and charged for it. A Green-Court herb-tender who had blessed a salve now sold salves in a market.

The Courts did not, so far as anyone can tell, object. But the drift left a residue. Every smith today owes a devotional debt they did not knowingly take on. Every alchemist's vial is, technically, a libation vessel. The old forms survive as superstitions — a smith who taps the anvil three times before the first heat is not being quaint; a great-great-grandparent of theirs was invoking the Hearth-Court, and the tap still works, and nobody is sure whether the Court is listening the way it used to.

The Bronze Tide also brought the **Sea-League Wars** — a century of city-states and island confederations fighting over tin, harbors, and Court-patronage. The wars ended in the **Salt Concordat**: free passage between harbors, hospitality for any sworn traveler, and the neutrality of the oracular orders.

### Living memory — *the last three generations*

- **Forty years ago:** The **Year the Sun Stuttered.** For three days at high summer the Dawn-Court went silent. Crops confused, oaths sworn in that window are still considered dubious, and every person over fifty remembers where they were. No priest has a satisfactory explanation. Several have satisfactory-sounding ones, which is worse.
- **Twenty years ago:** The **Bronze Wyrm of Hail Reach** — a mythic beast long thought extinct — surfaced, sank two league-ships, and was killed by a coalition of heroes who have since quarreled over the killing blow. The song is still being argued. At least three living men and one woman claim the title "Killer of the Bronze Wyrm," and at least two are lying.
- **Within the last five years:** A rising number of small, local oath-failures. Smiths whose best work goes dull. Fishers whose reliable runs have thinned. Oracles whose dreams come back muddled. No single cause named. The priestly lineages blame each other. The Courts, as ever, are not explaining themselves.

This is the pressure the present sits under.

## 4. Geography & Material Economy

### The shape of the known world

The setting is the **Kelpmouth Reach** — a crescent of four large islands and some thirty smaller ones wrapped around a shallow, warm inner sea called the **Lap**, with a single mainland headland (the **Ridge**) curving in from the south like a thumb. At local scale this is roughly a hundred tiles across: you can walk from the Ridge's southernmost shrine to the northern cliff-villages in a long, hard season; you can sail the Lap corner-to-corner in nine days with fair wind.

The Lap is sunlit, bronze-warm, and shallow enough in many places that a galley's keel scrapes coral at low tide. Beyond the outer islands, the **Long Dark Water** begins — the open sea where the Unhoused are said to still bargain, and where no sensible league-captain sails without a Veilwise aboard.

### Biomes in five strokes

- **The Lap** — inner sea, warm, shoal-threaded, heavy with fish and salt-trade.
- **The Ridge** — the mainland spur. Olive-scrub, limestone karst, river mouths, the only deep forests within reach.
- **The Spine Isles** — the four big islands, each with a central upland of bare stone under which the Stone-Court is said to sleep lightly.
- **The Thresh** — a belt of small low islands across the Lap's southern mouth, sandbars and reed-flats where the Green-Court is strongest.
- **The Cold Shoulder** — the northernmost islands, where the Rime-Court still leaves frost on midsummer rigging and the fishing is best and the oaths are strictest.

### Scarcities that shape everything

Tin is the axis. The Reach has copper in abundance (Ridge mines, two of the Spine Isles) but only one reliable tin source — the **Pale Seams** on the Cold Shoulder's inner coast. Every bronze blade, cauldron, oar-lock, and temple-gong in the Reach owes something to a cold, grudging northern league that knows exactly what its tin is worth. The Bronze Tide was a bronze tide because of the Pale Seams; the Sea-League Wars were fought over them; the Salt Concordat exists because a war over them could not be finished.

Secondary scarcities: deep-water shipbuilding timber (the Ridge only; island hulls are pieced); the salt grade required for certain Green-Court rites (the Thresh only); the black sand for T3+ enchanting glass (one beach, contested).

### The four tiers, read materially

- **T1 — *Common-weight.*** Honest work by a tradesperson whose oaths are intact enough. A T1 bronze knife is a good knife; it will take an edge, hold it through a season's hard use. No Court attends its making. It does not sing.
- **T2 — *Named-work.*** The maker's name is on it, in the old sense — struck, scored, or sung in during the last heat. A T2 fish-hook, pulled from a river, remembers whose hand set it and will go slack for a thief. A T2 bronze sword parries a little truer than a T1. One Court witnessed the making, usually through a small household rite.
- **T3 — *Lineage-work.*** Made inside an unbroken lineage, at a station whose instruments themselves are named. A T3 ingot is refined in a smelter whose fire has not been fully extinguished in three generations. A T3 potion is brewed in a vessel that has held only its own kind of work. Two Courts attend, most often. A T3 item can be felt across a room by anyone with Name-Sight.
- **T4 — *Oath-witnessed.*** These are not simply better. They are *rare, remembered events*. A T4 bronze spear is forged under open sky with three breathing witnesses, a named oath spoken over the last heat, and at least one Court formally invoked. A T4 fish is landed during a public vow-pilgrimage — the fish is the vow's answer, and may not be eaten casually; some are kept in salt for generations as reliquaries. A T4 enchantment-stone is set in a public rite where the maker swears to never unmake it; unmaking is possible, and undoes standing back three generations on the maker's side.

T4 is not something a workshop produces. It is something a life produces, sometimes once, sometimes never.

### Where the Courts lean

- **Stone** — strongest in the upland Spines and the Ridge's karst; weakest at sea.
- **Hearth** — strongest in homes and established forges; follows habitation, not terrain.
- **Rime** — the Cold Shoulder is practically her courtyard; almost absent in the Thresh.
- **Lash** — the open Lap in storm season, high places, exposed ridgelines.
- **Green** — the Thresh, river mouths, the Ridge's deep forests.
- **Veilwise** — libraries, archives, crossroads, and very old harbors where too many names have been spoken.
- **Undersong** — grave-islands, the Long Dark Water, any threshold.
- **Dawn** — public squares, league-moots, the decks of ships in full sun.

No Court is absent anywhere. They are only *nearer* in some places. A smith on the Thresh can still invoke Hearth; she will simply work harder for the same attention.

### Trade routes

Three arteries define the Lap's economy: the **Tin Road** (Cold Shoulder → every harbor that can pay), the **Salt Loop** (Thresh ↔ Ridge ↔ Spines, circular), and the **Hull Run** (Ridge → Spine shipwrights). Leagues fight, quietly, over stations along each.

## 5. Peoples & Cultures

Five peoples share the Reach. None is monolithic; each is a tendency.

### The Pale-Seam Kindreds (Cold Shoulder)

The tin-holders. Clans rather than cities — six named kindreds share the Pale Seams under rotating custodianship. They live in long stone houses whose hearths are never let die. Oath-keeping is hypertrophied here: a Pale-Seam adult by thirty has sworn perhaps forty public oaths and remembers every one. They favor the **Rime-Court** (patience, preservation) and the **Stone-Court** (the seams themselves). Their taboo: *no oath spoken twice.* To re-swear a prior oath is to admit the first was weak, and the Rime-Court, who remembers, is insulted. Sample names: Horl of Black-Tooth, Ivenna Seam-Keeper, Dugr Nine-Winter. Their speech is short, pauseful, and consonant-heavy.

### The Lapfolk (inner sea, Spine harbors)

The sailors, traders, musicians, and most of the league-captains. Polyglot, mercantile, fond of song-contests and public vows. They winter in Spine harbors, summer on the water. They favor the **Dawn-Court** (public oaths), the **Lash-Court** (bold strokes), and — privately — the **Undersong Court** (the dead they've left in the Long Dark Water). Their virtue: *cleverness that pays its debts.* A Lapfolk trader who cheats is not shamed for cheating; they are shamed for getting the accounts wrong after. Names are long, sung, and often include a parent's harbor: Suli of Three-Gulls-Harbor, Maren Tallwake, Ossen Bright-of-Tongue. Lapfolk culture is the default *sound* of the Reach.

### The Ridge-Houses (mainland headland)

Agriculturalists, foresters, shipwrights, and the region's only real cavalry. Older than the Sea-League Wars; they predate the Bronze Tide. Live in fortified hill-houses above olive terraces. They favor the **Green-Court** (the deep forest, the orchard, the poison-that-heals) and the **Stone-Court** (the karst their houses sit on). Their taboo: *no guest unfed past sundown.* A Ridge-House that sends a guest out hungry loses standing with every Court at once — hospitality is older than the Courts. Names are paired: given name + house-name, the house-name always a feature of the land. Dara of Oakfall, Perrin of White-Scarp, Jole of Nineoaks. They speak slowly and distrust people who don't.

### The Thresh-Walkers (southern reed-islands)

Not a nation. A loose confederation of mud-dwellers, salt-gatherers, reed-weavers, and Green-Court herbalists. They build on stilts and move their villages every decade or so as the sandbars shift. They favor the **Green-Court** (uncontested) and the **Undersong Court** (the drowned things, the Unhoused rumored to lie in the Thresh's deep channels). Their virtue: *speaking the true name of an appetite.* Thresh-Walker culture treats hunger, lust, fear, and grief as things you name out loud, to the Green-Court, before acting on them — otherwise the Green takes a larger cut. Sample names: Eshka Reedmother, Thil Under-the-Sand, Nen Who-Named-the-Eel. Outsiders find them uncanny. They are uncanny.

### The Veilwise Orders (pan-Reach, no single territory)

Not a people — a caste. The Veilwise Orders are the scholar-diviners, archivists, and name-keepers who operate under Salt Concordat neutrality in every significant harbor. They are drawn from all four other peoples, adopted young, and thereafter belong to their order first. They keep the registers of oaths, name-sign the T4 makings, and arbitrate when leagues quarrel. They favor, of course, the **Veilwise Court**, and are forbidden — by the Concordat — from favoring any other publicly. They take single-word chosen names on induction: Kell, Sima, Oren, Warden-of-the-Eighth-Stair. Their current corruption is discussed in Section 6.

## 6. Institutions

Six priestly-craft lineages and two trans-regional orders. Each has a founding myth and a current corruption. The six crafting disciplines map to the six lineages.

### The Anvil-Keepers *(Smithing → Hearth-Court / Stone-Court)*

**Founding myth.** In the Long First Morning, the first smith — **Brenda Cold-Forge** — lit her fire with a coal brought up from the Stone-Court's own hearth at the world's root, and swore to tend that fire, and her daughters after her, so long as the Hearth-Court would bless the iron. The oldest Anvil-Keeper forges still hold a coal descended, in the telling, from that first coal — never fully quenched across generations.

**Current corruption.** The largest Anvil-Keeper house on the Spines, the **Forge of Black Gulls**, let its coal die six years ago during a lineage-quarrel and relit it from a neighbor's flame. They have not told the Hearth-Court. Their T3 work still passes inspection. Most of it. Some of it has started to fail in ways their own journeymen cannot explain and will not admit.

### The Salt-Brewers *(Alchemy → Green-Court)*

**Founding myth.** The Green-Court taught the first herbalist, **Oma of the Fever Year**, that medicine and venom are a single Court in different moods, and that the measure of an alchemist is the honesty with which she names which mood she is working in. Every Salt-Brewer vial today bears a mark: the left-hand mark if the brew is to heal, the right-hand mark if it is to harm. Marking wrong is the original sin of the trade.

**Current corruption.** A Thresh-based brewing house, the **Reedmouth Consort**, has for a generation been supplying the Lapfolk leagues with dual-marked vials — right-handed in effect, left-handed in label — for use in mercantile disputes. The Green-Court, so far, has taken no obvious revenge. Some Salt-Brewers think this is because the Green-Court is patient. Others think it is because the Green-Court approves, which is worse.

### The Smelter-Priests *(Refining → Stone-Court / Lash-Court)*

**Founding myth.** To refine an ore is to call the Stone-Court to argue with the Lash-Court — the slow weight that wants to stay, and the bright stroke that wants to break. The first Smelter-Priest, **Orun Two-Hands**, stood between them for a full day and a full night and brokered the first ingot; the pouring of every ingot since is an echo of that arbitration. Smelter-Priests wear ash on one hand and soot on the other, always.

**Current corruption.** The Pale-Seam Kindreds have, for two generations, refused to let non-kindred Smelter-Priests work the Seam tin. Tin leaving the Cold Shoulder is now refined exclusively by the Kindreds' own priests, who (some whisper) have begun taking shortcuts — skipping the night-long vigil, pouring in half a day. The tin is still good. For now.

### The Keel-Wrights *(Engineering → Lash-Court / Stone-Court)*

**Founding myth.** The first hulls were gifts: a Ridge-House shipwright, **Tannen of Oakfall**, was taught the principle of the true keel by the Lash-Court itself in a dream, on condition that no keel be laid without witness. Every Keel-Wright hull still goes into the water witnessed — by the wright, a league-officer, and a child under twelve whose memory will carry the hull's name forward when everyone else is dead.

**Current corruption.** The **Brightwake Yard** on the largest Spine has begun laying keels in covered sheds, without the child-witness, to meet Lapfolk demand. The hulls sail. The song has thinned, though — Brightwake ships are not remembered by name, and sailors who serve on them find their own names going softer in the ears of the Courts.

### The Name-Binders *(Enchanting → Veilwise Court / Dawn-Court)*

**Founding myth.** To enchant is to bind a true name into a made thing, and the first Name-Binder — **Sima the First** (every head of the order takes "Sima" as her name) — swore before the Veilwise and Dawn Courts together that no name would be bound against its bearer's will, and no binding would be unmade in secret. The binding of a T4 enchantment today still requires the name's bearer to speak it aloud in open sun, before witnesses.

**Current corruption.** The Name-Binders' order is, formally, incorruptible; Veilwise neutrality forbids political work. In practice, the order has begun accepting — quietly — commissions from specific Lapfolk leagues to bind names into trade-seals, which effectively creates monopolies enforceable by Court-standing. The bindings are technically clean. The use is not.

### The Net-Singers *(Fishing → Undersong Court / Rime-Court)*

**Founding myth.** The first Net-Singer, **Vessa Long-Breath**, was taught by the Undersong Court that every fish carries a share of an ancestor's unfinished business, and that to fish is to negotiate with the dead. Net-Singers sing a verse of genealogy — usually the fish-house's own — over every significant catch, returning one in ten to the water named. The returned fish is the Undersong's share.

**Current corruption.** The largest Lapfolk fishing-houses have, across the last decade, quietly reduced the returned share from one-in-ten to one-in-thirty, claiming the leaner years require it. The catches remain strong. The dreams of the fishers' children, reportedly, do not.

### The Veilwise Orders *(trans-regional, see Section 5)*

**Founding myth.** At the signing of the Salt Concordat, the Veilwise swore to hold neutral the registry of oaths and names across all leagues, taking no favor from any Court but their own. They are the reason a Pale-Seam oath can be enforced in a Thresh market.

**Current corruption.** Certain Veilwise archivists on the busiest Spine have begun losing records — specifically, records of oaths inconvenient to the Brightwake Yard, the Forge of Black Gulls, and two major Lapfolk leagues. The losses are subtle. The **Warden-of-the-Eighth-Stair** knows. She has not yet decided what she is.

### The League-Moots *(civic, pan-Reach)*

**Founding myth.** The Salt Concordat established a rotating moot — delegates from every signatory league and kindred meet once every three years on a neutral Spine harbor to renew the Concordat's oaths in open sun. The Dawn-Court attends; this is the largest public rite in the Reach.

**Current corruption.** The last three moots have been shorter than the three before them. Delegates arrive later, leave earlier. The renewal oath is spoken; the feast afterward is thinner every cycle. The Dawn-Court, asked directly by a junior priest whether the thinning registers with her, declined to answer. This is — priests agree, privately — the worst of the omens.

## 7. Central Tensions (Fronts)

Four Fronts, each a slow pressure the fiction is asking a question about, each with named actors and a clock. They interact: advancing one often advances another.

### Front I — The Saltless Years

**Stakes question.** *Will the Rime-Court withdraw her patience from the Cold Shoulder, and if she does, does the Reach's tin supply survive it?*

The small oath-failures of the last five years (see Section 3) are most concentrated in the Pale-Seam Kindreds — the people who remember the most oaths and have therefore the most oaths to fray. The Rime-Court, who rewards patience and remembers every grievance, has been subtly withdrawing: midsummer frosts on inland tin-barges, dreams of the dead that arrive a half-beat late, a kindred-matriarch who swore an oath three years ago and cannot, now, remember the exact words. The Smelter-Priest shortcuts (Section 6) make it worse. The Kindreds blame the Lapfolk leagues, who are buying tin faster than it can be properly refined.

**Named actors.** Ivenna Seam-Keeper (matriarch, Horl Kindred, trying to hold the old forms); Dugr Nine-Winter (her rival, arguing for faster refining to meet demand); the Rime-Court itself, silent.

**Clock.** Six stages: Grievance remembered → First bad batch → Kindred quarrel open → Rime-Court sign withdrawn (e.g., the Seams frost over in summer) → First tin-famine year → The Seams close. Currently at stage two.

### Front II — The Thinning Moot

**Stakes question.** *Does the Salt Concordat still mean what it meant, and if the Dawn-Court stops attending the renewal, what holds the Reach together?*

The League-Moots have thinned (Section 6). The Dawn-Court's silence on the question of whether she still attends is itself the omen. If the next moot passes with the renewal oath spoken to an absent Court, the Concordat becomes a piece of paper — and every league that has been quietly cheating becomes free to cheat openly. The Brightwake Yard, the Forge of Black Gulls, the Reedmouth Consort, and two major Lapfolk trading houses all have motive to let the Concordat hollow.

**Named actors.** Ossen Bright-of-Tongue (Lapfolk league-captain, publicly Concordat-loyal, privately the biggest beneficiary of its thinning); Warden-of-the-Eighth-Stair (Veilwise archivist, knows where the missing oath-records are); Dara of Oakfall (Ridge-House elder, the last delegate to walk out of a moot in visible protest); the Dawn-Court, not answering.

**Clock.** Five stages: First missing record → Second skipped delegate → First failed enforcement → Dawn-Court absence at next moot → Concordat collapse. Currently at stage two. The next moot is eighteen months out.

### Front III — The Bronze Wyrm's Quarrel

**Stakes question.** *When more than one hero claims the same deed, which song does the world choose to remember — and what does the Undersong Court do with the names of the rivals?*

The killing of the Bronze Wyrm of Hail Reach is twenty years old and still contested. Three living men and one living woman publicly claim the killing blow. At least two are lying. The Veilwise Orders have refused, unusually, to adjudicate — the records from that expedition were lost (some say destroyed) in a harbor fire the same year. The Undersong Court, patron of what is true but not said, has begun visiting the four claimants in their sleep. One has gone quiet and stopped claiming the title; one has doubled down and taken to wearing a wyrm-scale pendant; one is drinking; one — the woman, **Maren Tallwake**, a Lapfolk league-captain — has announced she will sail to Hail Reach at midsummer to "settle the account," which no one knows how to interpret.

**Named actors.** Maren Tallwake; Perrin of White-Scarp (Ridge-House claimant, drinking); Jole of Nineoaks (Ridge-House claimant, pendant-wearer); Ossen Bright-of-Tongue (yes — the same; his claim is the suspected-false one); the Undersong Court, dreaming.

**Clock.** Four stages: Claimant quiet → Claimant breaks down → Midsummer reckoning → The song settles (one name remembered, three forgotten — and forgetting, to the Undersong, is a kind of second death). Currently at stage two.

### Front IV — What Rises in the Thresh

**Stakes question.** *Are the Unhoused Courts — the drowned, exiled ones — waking, and if so, who among the living has been bargaining with them?*

The Thresh-Walkers have, this past year, reported three new things: a channel that was shallow is now fathoms deep; a song no living walker was taught is being hummed by children; Eshka Reedmother dreamed of a Court with no name that offered her healing for her granddaughter in exchange for "only a small oath, nothing you would miss." She refused. Nen Who-Named-the-Eel did not refuse the same Court's same offer, and his granddaughter is well, and Nen will not meet anyone's eyes.

The Reedmouth Consort's deliberate mis-marking of vials (Section 6) is suspected, by at least one Veilwise, of being an Unhoused bargain: the Green-Court looks away because an older, hungrier Court has been paid to ask her to.

**Named actors.** Eshka Reedmother (refused the bargain, will not be quiet about it); Nen Who-Named-the-Eel (took the bargain, will not speak of it); the Reedmouth Consort (suspected conduit); an Unhoused Court, *not named here because naming it is a thread pulled*.

**Clock.** Five stages: Isolated bargains → Pattern visible to outsiders → An Unhoused name spoken aloud in public → A Housed Court reacts → What the reaction costs. Currently at stage two.

### How the Fronts interact

The Saltless Years stresses the Pale-Seam Kindreds; stressed Kindreds refine tin faster; fast-refined tin makes cheaper bronze; cheap bronze empowers the Lapfolk leagues who are hollowing the Concordat. The Bronze Wyrm's Quarrel names Ossen Bright-of-Tongue, who is also the league-captain most benefiting from the Thinning Moot. The Reedmouth Consort (Thresh Front) supplies vials to those same leagues. A player who pulls one thread pulls all four.

## 8. Human-Scale Texture & Negative Space

### What a day feels like

**A greeting.** Two Lapfolk meeting on a dock clasp the left forearm, not the hand — the right hand is for oaths, and unsworn greetings don't touch it. A Ridge-House greeting adds the house-name: "Dara of Oakfall greets you." A Pale-Seam greeting is a single word, the guest's name, said flat. A Thresh-Walker greeting is a question about the tide.

**A meal.** A share of the first bite goes to the hearth — not to a god, to *the hearth itself*, because the Hearth-Court is jealous and lives in the fire that cooked the food. Bread is torn, not cut. Fish, if Net-Sung, has a bone returned to the water at the end of the meal — even a meal inland, into a bowl of saltwater kept for the purpose. Wine is poured with the left hand; wine poured with the right is an oath-wine, and someone had better be about to swear something.

**A market.** Loud. Sunlit. Weights are sworn on — every scale in a Reach market is registered with the local Veilwise, and the vendor touches the scale with the right hand before the first weighing of the day, which is a small silent oath to weigh true. A vendor caught thumbing the scale is not fined; the scale is taken from her and not returned for a year, and everyone knows why. T2 goods are marked openly. T3 goods are kept in back and shown only to buyers the vendor knows. T4 goods are not sold; they are *given*, against a later oath, and the giving is itself a rite.

**A wedding.** Outdoors, in sun, three witnesses minimum — the Dawn-Court's bare requirement. The couple swear two oaths: the first, said together, is the oath to each other; the second, said separately, is the oath to the marriage's *future ancestors*, the unborn, whose weight they are promising to carry. A widow or widower remarrying swears a third oath to the first spouse, apologizing and asking permission; the Undersong Court attends this one, and the attending is felt.

**A funeral.** Names are spoken once, loudly, by someone who loved the dead. Then not spoken again for a year and a day. The Undersong Court takes the name in trust for that year; pulling it back early — speaking the dead's name in anger, in song, in a tavern — is the third forbidden thing (Section 2). After a year, the name returns to common use, and the dead becomes an ancestor, weight that can be leaned on.

**A contract.** Signed in front of Veilwise witnesses on a registered slip of vellum. The slip is split: the buyer's half, the seller's half. Re-joining the two halves (an act called *the matching*) is how a dispute is resolved — if the halves no longer match, one party has altered theirs, and the Veilwise can see that from across the room because altered vellum *smells wrong* to Name-Sight.

**A curse.** Casual cursing exists — "by the Hearth!" is as common as the English "damn" — but *naming* a Court in anger is different. A Lapfolk dockworker who snarls "Lash take you!" at a rival has said something real, and the rival is entitled to demand a public retraction. Most days, most of the time, nothing happens. Sometimes — reportedly — the Lash does take someone, and that is the story told for a generation.

### What is deliberately left unspecified

A lorebook is more honest when it names its gaps. The following are chosen holes, not lazy ones, and each is chosen because the setting works better if the answer isn't fixed:

- **The fate of the Unhoused Courts.** Drowned? Dormant? Still bargaining? The songs disagree and priests have different theories, and a campaign or story set in the Reach should be free to decide this for its own purposes.
- **The true count of original Courts.** Twelve, nineteen, thirty-three — the songs disagree and the Veilwise have been quietly destroying the count-records for centuries, for reasons they will not explain.
- **Whether the First Oath is actually unbreakable.** It has never been tested in living memory. Every priest suspects it could be broken. No priest will be the one to test it. This is a tension the setting keeps live.
- **What the Dawn-Court's three-day silence forty years ago actually was.** No satisfactory explanation exists. Every explanation is a potential plot.
- **Whether the Courts are conscious in a human sense or merely vast appetites with rules.** This matters enormously and is never resolved in-setting; scholars argue both ways, and the Courts decline to clarify.
- **Lands beyond the Reach.** The Long Dark Water holds other shores. They are not described. Travelers from them exist and are rare and strange; what they are like is a gap a later pass or a specific story can fill.
- **The actual mechanism of enchantment-binding at T4.** We know it requires name, sun, witnesses, and a vow; we do not specify the exact liturgy, because no two Name-Binder houses perform it the same and the variance is part of their mystique.

### Titles — a note on heroic epithets

The ~40 titles of Game-1 map, in this setting, to earned heroic epithets in the old style: *Seam-Keeper*, *Long-Breath*, *Nine-Winter*, *Killer of the Bronze Wyrm*, *Reedmother*, *Under-the-Sand*, *Bright-of-Tongue*, *Two-Hands*, *Cold-Forge*, *Tallwake*, *Who-Named-the-Eel*. They are not granted by authorities. They are earned by deed and confirmed by song — which means the Veilwise eventually registers them, which means the Courts eventually recognize them, which means a titled person can spend standing on a scale an untitled one cannot. To lose a title (through disproof, through oath-failure, through a song that turns) is a specific and nameable disgrace: one becomes *Ill-Sung*, and Ill-Sung is its own kind of social weight.

## Appendix — Open Questions for the User

The framework is ready to use, but five decisions are worth your signal before any downstream work (character generation, quest seeding, faction prompts, WES tool integrations). Each is a live gap, not a placeholder:

1. **Are the Unhoused Courts still conscious bargainers, or echoes?** The Thresh Front (IV) currently treats them as active. But the tone changes sharply depending: conscious Unhoused make this a cosmic horror adjacent to sunlit fantasy; echo-only Unhoused make it a story about mortals corrupting themselves with ritual fragments. I've written it to support either, but the tone and the pacing of Front IV tighten fast once you pick. Which do you want?

2. **How explicit should Court personalities be in mechanical terms?** Right now a Court is a character (Rime "patient as widowhood," Lash "loves a bold stroke"). The game's existing combat tags (fire, ice, etc.) would map naturally to Court-flavored damage, but I've avoided stat-ifying the Courts themselves. Do you want Courts to have numerical standing thresholds (e.g., "Rime withdraws at -20 standing"), or stay entirely qualitative / narrative?

3. **Is the player a member of one of the five peoples, or an outsider?** Massive difference in starting oath-debt, family pressure, and Court-affinity defaults. A Pale-Seam player starts under forty sworn oaths and a clan obligation; a Lapfolk player starts with a personal ledger and no clan; a Thresh-Walker player is already uncanny. The whole first hour of play feels different per choice. Do you want one canonical starting people, player's choice at char creation, or a default of "outsider from beyond the Reach"?

4. **How central should the Bronze Wyrm's Quarrel (Front III) be?** It's the most narratively ready Front — named rivals, midsummer deadline, quick to dramatize. But it risks becoming the main plot by default. Should I treat it as the starting emergency (explicit main arc), one of four equally weighted Fronts, or background color a player can ignore entirely?

5. **What's beyond the Long Dark Water?** I deliberately left this blank (Section 8), but it's the single biggest potential tonal intrusion: if outsiders from beyond arrive in play, their strangeness can either deepen the sunlit-mythic register (they seem *older*, like the Long First Morning returning) or rupture it (they seem *modern*, industrial, alien to the oath-economy). Do you have a preference, or want me to leave it permanently unwritten and write around it?
